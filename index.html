Designing a fitness program

Five Components of Fitness
  • 1. BODY COMPOSITION:
    This isn't just about weight; it's about what makes up that weight. Body composition is the proportion of fat and non-fat in your body. It provides insights into your fitness level and potential health risks. A good body composition means having a good balance of body fat and muscle. We will talk more about this later.
  • 2. CARDIORESPIRATORY FITNESS:
    This is the powerhouse combo of your heart and lungs. It gauges how efficiently they work together to fuel your muscles with oxygen during prolonged physical activities. Boosting cardiorespiratory fitness means enhancing your body's ability to take in oxygen and deliver it to your cells, making activities like running, swimming, or cycling easier and more enjoyable.
  • 3. FLEXIBILITY:
    It's not just about touching your toes! Flexibility is the range of motion in a joint or group of joints. Regular stretching can improve flexibility, which in turn improves your performance in physical activities, decreases your risk of injuries, and ensures your joints remain healthy.
  • 4. MUSCULAR ENDURANCE:
    This is all about stamina. It determines how long your muscles can sustain an activity before they get tired. For instance, how many squats or push-ups can you do in a row? Activities like rowing, climbing, or circuit training can boost muscular endurance, ensuring you can keep going for longer without feeling fatigued.
  • 5. MUSCULAR STRENGTH:
    This is the raw power your muscles can generate. It's about how much force they can exert in a single effort. Building muscular strength involves resistance training, like weightlifting or resistance band exercises. Stronger muscles not only help in daily tasks but also protect your joints from injuries.

Benefits of Exercise

PHYSICAL BENEFITS

Cardiovascular Health: Improves heart health by increasing heart efficiency and reducing the risk of heart disease.

Weight Management: Helps burn calories, which can aid in weight loss and prevent weight gain.

Muscle Strength and Endurance: Strengthens muscles and improves endurance.

Bone Health: Weight-bearing exercises can increase bone density and reduce the risk of osteoporosis.

Improved Respiratory Function: Increases lung capacity and efficiency.

Reduced Risk of Chronic Diseases: Such as type 2 diabetes, certain cancers, and high blood pressure.

Boosted Immunity: Moderate exercise can boost the immune system by improving overall general health.

Hormonal Balance: Helps regulate hormones, which can improve mood, sleep, and reduce the risk of chronic diseases.

Flexibility: Stretching exercises can improve flexibility, reducing the risk of injuries.

Improved Digestion: Can aid in better digestion and reduce the risk of constipation.

MENTAL BENEFITS

Stress Reduction: Physical activity increases the production of endorphins, which are natural mood lifters.

Mood Enhancement: Can alleviate feelings of depression and anxiety.

Improved Sleep: Regular physical activity can help regulate sleep patterns.

Boosted Self-esteem: Physical fitness can boost self-esteem and improve self-image.

Enhanced Cognitive Function: Exercise can improve brain function and protect memory and thinking skills.

Reduction in Cognitive Decline: Can reduce the risk of developing conditions like Alzheimer's disease.

SOCIAL BENEFITS

Improved Social Skills: Group exercises and team sports can help improve social skills.

Community Engagement: Joining a gym or a sports club can lead to increased community engagement.

Building Relationships: Shared physical activities can strengthen relationships with friends and family.

LIFESTYLE BENEFITS

Increased Energy: Regular physical activity can boost stamina and reduce feelings of fatigue.

Better Posture: Strength training and flexibility exercises can improve posture.

Enhanced Performance: Improved physical health can lead to enhanced performance in daily activities and tasks.

Longevity: Regular exercise is associated with a longer lifespan.

OTHER BENEFITS

Enhanced Creativity: Some people find that they are more creative after a workout.

Better Skin Health: By increasing blood flow, exercise can help nourish skin cells and keep them healthy.

Pain Management: Can help in the management and reduction of pain, including chronic pain.


FITT Formula

F - FREQUENCY:
Depending on what you’re trying to do, frequency can change. If you are doing cardo and trying to burn fat, your body doesn’t need as much recovery, so you can do it more often, something like 5 times a week works. Or if you’re trying to build muscle/ lift weights, 2-3 can work. Start slow to avoid injury and gradually increase the frequency as your body adapts.

I - INTENSITY:
Pay attention to your heart rate, make sure it stays under control. For strength training, start with weights that you can lift for 12-15 reps without straining, then gradually increase the weight as you become stronger. Focus on form and don’t increase the weight until your form is near perfect, ego lifting hurts your gains and can cause injury.

T - TIME:
Aim for a minimum of 20 minutes per session. For cardiovascular workouts, 20-30 minutes is a good starting point, but you can increase this as your stamina improves. For strength training, the duration might vary based on the number of sets and exercises you're doing, but ensuring you're working out each muscle group adequately is key. Try not to overload yourself, maintaining motivation is just as important as maintaining gains.

T - TYPE:
Again, this will vary depending on your goals. To burn fat, engage in aerobic exercise. Aerobic exercises, such as walking, jogging, cycling, and swimming, are great for improving cardiovascular health, burning calories, and boosting mood. Now, for strength training, in addition to lifting, don’t forget to perform flexibility exercises, and balance workouts. This ensures all muscle groups are worked, reduces the risk of injury, and improves overall functional fitness.

Reading a food label

Understanding Food Labels

The FDA requires a nutrition facts label on most packaged foods and drinks in the U.S.

Most food labels will look like figure A. However, if the container has more than one serving but typically might be consumed in one sitting, such as a pint of ice cream, the label will have two additional columns like in label B.

    What are all these things?

    At a glace
  • If you don't have time to read the entire label, skimming over these parts will be helpful:

    • 1. Serving size and servings per container
    Don't let this fool you! Everything on the label is based on serving size, there can be many servings in the entire container. This product has 8 servings, each being a 2/3 cup.

    2. Calories
    What your looking for here may vary from person to person, but most people should be eating 2,000 to 2,500 calories a day (we will talk more about this later), and the label shows calories per serving.

    3. Basic nutrition
    The less saturated fat the better, keep away from high sugar or sodium content, this will vary from person to person as well.

    Everything you need to know
  • Serving Size: The average amount people should consume at once.
  • Calories: The energy you get from a serving of the food.
  • Daily Values:

    Calories 2,000 2,500
    Total Fat Less than 65g Less than 80g
    Sat Fat Less than 20g Less than 30g
    Cholesterol Less than 300mg Less than 300mg
    Sodium Less than 2,400mg Less than 2,400mg
    Total Carbohydrate 300g 375g
    Dietary Fiber 25g 30g
    Again, specific values will vary from person to person. Talk to your doctor or a professional at least once in your life.
  • Vitamins and Minerals: Essential nutrients your body needs.
    • Fat-Soluble Vitamins (4):
      1. Vitamin A: Essential for vision, immune function, and skin health. Found in foods like carrots and sweet potatoes.
      2. Vitamin D: Vital for bone health and calcium absorption. Synthesized by the skin when exposed to sunlight.
      3. Vitamin E: Acts as an antioxidant, protecting cells from damage. Found in nuts and vegetable oils.
      4. Vitamin K: Necessary for blood clotting and bone metabolism. Abundant in leafy greens like spinach.
    • Water-Soluble Vitamins (9):
      1. Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid): Boosts the immune system and aids in collagen production. Found in citrus fruits.
      2. Vitamin B1 (Thiamine): Supports energy metabolism and nerve function. Commonly found in whole grains.
      3. Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin): Essential for energy production and healthy skin. Found in dairy products.
      4. Vitamin B3 (Niacin): Important for metabolism and skin health. Present in poultry and peanuts.
      5. Vitamin B5 (Pantothenic Acid): Involved in fatty acid synthesis and energy production. Found in many foods.
      6. Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine): Necessary for brain development and function. Abundant in meats and beans.
      7. Vitamin B7 (Biotin): Supports healthy hair, skin, and nails. Found in eggs and nuts.
      8. Vitamin B9 (Folate): Crucial for DNA synthesis and cell growth. Commonly found in leafy greens.
      9. Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin): Essential for nerve function and red blood cell production. Mainly found in animal products.
    • Minerals (3):
      1. Calcium: Important for bone health and various bodily processes.
      2. Sodium (Salt): Essential for maintaining fluid balance and nerve function.
      3. Copper: Necessary for enzyme activity and overall health.
  • Good Fats: Polyunsaturated fats and monounsaturated fats are beneficial for heart health and overall well-being when consumed in moderation and used to replace saturated or trans fats in the diet.
  • Bad Fats: Saturated fats and trans fats can negatively affect cardiovascular health, so it's recommended to limit their intake.
  • Ingredient List: Check the ingredients to see what's in the food product. Ingredients are typically listed in descending order by weight.
  • Cholesterol: Look for the cholesterol content, especially if you're concerned about heart health. High levels of cholesterol can contribute to heart disease.
  • Sugars: Pay attention to the amount of sugars listed. This includes both natural and added sugars. High sugar consumption can impact your overall health.
  • Fats: Check the type of fats present, such as saturated and trans fats. Limit foods high in saturated and trans fats as they can raise your risk of heart disease.

Choosing a Healthy Diet

Guidelines for Healthful Choices

Adopting a nutritious diet doesn't need to be overwhelming. Small, consistent choices can lead to substantial health benefits. When shopping or dining out, being aware of the foods you choose is very important.

Simple Steps to a Nutritious Diet

Start by incorporating more fruits and vegetables into your meals, they provide essential vitamins and minerals. Opt for whole grains over refined grains for better sustained energy. Lean proteins, like fish or legumes, should be a staple, and try to limit the intake of sugary drinks and snacks. When possible, choose home-cooked meals over processed foods to control ingredients and portion sizes.

The Six Basic Nutrients

A balanced diet consists of six key nutrients.

  • Carbohydrates: The primary energy source, fueling both muscles and the brain.
  • Fats: Essential for nutrient absorption, nerve function, and energy storage.
  • Proteins: The building blocks for tissues, hormones, and enzymes in the body.
  • Vitamins: Crucial for a myriad of metabolic processes, cell function, and disease prevention.
  • Minerals: Play a role in bone health, enzyme production, and cardiovascular health.
  • Water: Vital for digestion, nutrient transportation, and temperature regulation.

Healthy Cooking at Home

Healthy Recipe: Quinoa & Veggie Salad

Ingredients:
  • 1 cup cooked quinoa
  • 1 cup mixed bell peppers, chopped
  • 1 cup cherry tomatoes, halved
  • 1/2 cucumber, diced
  • 1/4 cup feta cheese, crumbled (optional)
  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice
  • Salt and pepper to taste
Instructions:
  1. In a large mixing bowl, combine quinoa, bell peppers, tomatoes, and cucumber.
  2. In a separate small bowl, whisk together olive oil, lemon juice, salt, and pepper.
  3. Pour the dressing over the quinoa mixture and toss to combine.
  4. Garnish with feta cheese if desired.
  5. Serve chilled or at room temperature.
Basic Tips for Lower Fat & Cholesterol Content:
  • Grill or Bake: Opt for grilling or baking instead of frying.
  • Use Olive Oil: It's a healthier alternative to butter or lard.
  • Opt for Lean Meat: Choose cuts that have less visible fat.
  • Limit Cheese & Cream: They can be high in saturated fat.
  • Include More Vegetables: They add bulk and nutrition without the added fat.

Eating Disorders

Understanding Eating Disorders

Understanding eating disorders is crucial for both awareness and prevention. These conditions often stem from complex interactions between biological, behavioral, psychological, and social factors. They can have severe health consequences and can be fatal if not treated appropriately.

What are eating disorders?

Eating disorders are serious and often fatal illnesses that cause severe disturbances to a person's eating behaviors. Obsessions with food, body weight, and shape may also signal an eating disorder. These disorders can affect anyone, regardless of age, race, gender, or background.

Anorexia Nervosa vs. Bulimia Nervosa

Anorexia Nervosa: Characterized by weight loss, difficulties maintaining an appropriate body weight for height, age, and stature, and, in many individuals, distorted body image. People with anorexia generally restrict the number of calories and the types of food they eat.

Bulimia Nervosa: Involves episodes of eating large amounts of food followed by behaviors to prevent weight gain, such as forced vomiting. A feeling of being out of control during the binge-eating episodes is a hallmark of bulimia.

Muscle Dysmorphia

Often referred to as "bigorexia", muscle dysmorphia is a subtype of obsessive-compulsive disorder. It's characterized by an obsession with not being muscular enough. Those affected might spend long hours at the gym, obsessively focus on their diet, and frequently check their body in the mirror, but still perceive themselves as being too small.

It's essential to recognize the signs of these disorders and seek help if you or someone you know is struggling. Early intervention and treatment is crucial.
